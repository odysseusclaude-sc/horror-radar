User Personas

Four primary user archetypes who rely on Horror Radar to discover breakout indie horror games. Each persona drives specific feature priorities and navigation decisions.

Horror Radar Redesign Information Architecture April 2026
MK

Maya Kim

Content Creator / Horror YouTuber
75K subscribers · 3 years on YouTube
"I need to find the Lethal Company before everyone else does."
A Day in the Life
Maya checks Horror Radar every morning before recording. She filters for games released in the last 14 days with high OPS but low YouTube coverage — the sweet spot where quality meets opportunity. When she finds a candidate, she scans the Steam reviews for gameplay hooks she can turn into video titles, then races to publish before Insym or ManlyBadassHero beat her to it.
Goals
  • Find breakout games before larger channels cover them (first-mover advantage)
  • Assess whether a game has enough content for a full playthrough series
  • Track which games are gaining momentum so she can time her uploads
Pain Points
  • No way to see which games have low YouTube coverage but high quality (blind spots hidden in API)
  • Can't save a shortlist of candidates to compare later — has to screenshot or remember
  • Missing games that later blow up because she didn't check for 2 days
Key Metrics
YouTube coverage gap OPS 7-day trend Review velocity 48h early adoption Channel subscriber reach
Feature Wishlist
  • Blind spot view: games with high review scores but zero or minimal YouTube coverage
  • Watchlist with email/push alerts when a saved game's OPS spikes
  • YouTube coverage timeline showing which channels covered a game and when
DP

David Park

Publisher Scout / Business Development
Indie publisher · 6 years in games industry
"Show me games that are overperforming with zero marketing — that's where the opportunity is."
A Day in the Life
David reviews Horror Radar every Monday morning as part of his scouting pipeline. He sorts by OPS descending, then clicks into individual games to study developer profiles and velocity trajectories. When he spots a solo dev whose first game is pulling 90% positive reviews and climbing OPS, he adds them to his CRM and schedules an outreach email before a competing publisher notices.
Goals
  • Identify developers with breakout potential to offer publishing deals early
  • Compare game performance metrics side-by-side to prioritize outreach
  • Track OPS trajectory over weeks to distinguish genuine breakouts from spikes
Pain Points
  • Can't compare multiple games side-by-side — has to open each in a separate tab
  • Developer history and track record not visible from the main table
  • No CSV/API export to feed data into his existing scouting CRM
Key Metrics
OPS trajectory Developer track record Review sentiment % Decay retention Price × volume ratio
Feature Wishlist
  • Side-by-side comparison mode for 2-4 games with normalized charts
  • CSV export of filtered game lists with all metrics
  • Developer profile page showing their full game history and aggregate stats
RJ

Rachel Jensen

Steam Curator / Reddit Moderator
r/HorrorGaming mod · 2K curator followers
"My community trusts me to find the gems before they go mainstream."
A Day in the Life
Rachel browses Horror Radar twice a week, usually on Wednesday evenings and Sunday mornings. She focuses on review sentiment and community buzz signals — Reddit mention counts, Twitch viewer spikes, and whether a game has a demo. When she finds a strong candidate, she writes a curator review on Steam and shares a recommendation post on r/HorrorGaming, but currently has to manually copy every data point she wants to cite.
Goals
  • Surface high-quality games her community would enjoy, prioritizing sentiment over hype
  • Share curated recommendations with supporting data and context
  • Track games over time to write follow-up reviews after patches and updates
Pain Points
  • No share or export — has to screenshot metrics or manually type them into posts
  • Can't create or maintain lists of recommended games within Horror Radar
  • No way to track a game's trajectory after initial discovery (no notifications or watchlist)
Key Metrics
Review score % Reddit mentions Twitch streams Demo availability Community buzz composite
Feature Wishlist
  • One-click share card that generates a formatted image or link with key stats
  • Personal watchlist with custom notes and status tags (recommended, playing, backlog)
  • Weekly digest email summarizing new high-quality games and trending breakouts
AV

Alex Vasquez

Gaming Journalist / Freelance Writer
Freelance · Covers indie games for 3 outlets
"I need the data to tell the story — what's happening in indie horror right now?"
A Day in the Life
Alex visits Horror Radar once or twice a month when researching trend pieces. He dives deep into the Trends page, looking for subgenre momentum shifts, pricing patterns, and demo impact data that can anchor a data-driven article. When he finds a compelling angle — like psychological horror overtaking survival horror in OPS — he needs to pull specific numbers to cite, but currently has no way to export or deep-link to specific data views.
Goals
  • Identify market-level trends and subgenre shifts for data-driven articles
  • Pull specific, citable data points with timestamps for journalistic credibility
  • Find compelling breakout stories with clear before/after narratives
Pain Points
  • No CSV export — can't extract data for charts in his articles
  • Trends page is very long with no section navigation or anchor links for bookmarking
  • No historical comparison — can't see how this month compares to last month
Key Metrics
Subgenre momentum Market pulse 12-week Price intelligence Demo impact Breakout stories
Feature Wishlist
  • CSV/JSON export of any data view with date range filtering
  • Trends page section navigation with anchor links and table of contents
  • Historical snapshots — compare this month's market pulse to previous months
