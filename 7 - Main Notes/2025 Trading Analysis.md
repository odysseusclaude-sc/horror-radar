## Areas for Improvement

### 1. High-resistance day identification (before entry)
The only loss (6 Oct) and the low-R:R win (16 Oct) both came on days with overlapping macro headwinds (US Govt Shutdown, No News Driver). The journal itself flags this: "Identifying high resistance faster will be good." Consider building a pre-session checklist that explicitly scores conditions like: active government shutdown, no news drivers, bank holidays, prior large range days. If 2+ conditions are present, default to tape reading only.

### 2. The second trade trap
On 6 Oct, Trade 1 was a reasonable loss (5 handles). Trade 2 was impulsive — the thesis shifted mid-session and the second entry lost 8 more handles. The journal notes a 15-handle daily max loss rule, which worked. But avoiding the second trade entirely on a day that was already showing high resistance would save capital for better days.

### 3. Partial-taking strategy consistency
Early trades (Aug) used a structured partial system: take 1 contract off at the first target, 1 at the second, hold runners. By late October, many trades exit all 4 contracts at once. The 16 Oct trade (0.77 R:R, 7.5 handles) and 27 Oct trade (1.87 R:R, 8.875 handles) both had stops moved that closed the position prematurely. Consider whether a fixed partial structure (e.g., always take 2 off at first target, hold 2 as runners) would capture more of the available range on those days.

### 4. Premarket trading is new and untested
Starting from mid-October, trades were taken in the 0650-0710 premarket macro. While results so far are positive (17 Oct: 49h, 20 Oct: 35.6h, 27 Oct: 8.9h, 30 Oct: 97h), the sample size is small and premarket liquidity is thinner. Continue logging these separately and compare performance after 20+ premarket trades to see if the edge holds.

### 5. September had very few trades
Only 1 actual trade in September (29 Sep). While NFP discipline is admirable, the week of 8-10 Sep (post-NFP) also had no trades despite some setups being identified in the journal. The 8 Sep entry even notes a potential 60-handle winner that was just missed by 0.25 handles. Consider whether entry orders at C.E + 2 handles is too conservative for low-resistance days, and whether C.E alone might capture more of these near-misses.

## Recommendations for Improving the Setup

1. **Create a "Resistance Score" pre-session filter.** Before each session, score the day from 1-5 based on: news environment (NFP, CPI, PPI = high), prior day range, government shutdowns, bank holidays, no-news drivers. If score > 3, tape read only.

2. **Standardize partial exits.** Decide on a fixed structure and stick to it for at least 20 trades to gather clean data. Suggestion: 2 contracts at first target (minor BSL or PD array), 1 at second target (Terminus), 1 as runner with stop at breakeven.

3. **Track premarket vs AM session separately.** Create a field in the template (e.g., `session: premarket` or `session: AM`) to make it easy to compare performance across sessions after more data accumulates.

4. **Revisit the entry offset.** The 8 Sep near-miss (price came within 0.25 handles of the buy order) suggests the C.E + 2 handles offset might be too conservative. Test C.E + 1 or C.E alone for 10 trades and compare fill rates.

5. **Continue the quadrant analysis.** The October results strongly suggest that layering PD arrays above daily wick quadrant levels produces higher-quality entries. This is the most promising evolution in the setup.
