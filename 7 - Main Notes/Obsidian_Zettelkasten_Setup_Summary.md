# Obsidian Zettelkasten Setup & Philosophy

---

## The Philosophy of the System

The core principle is a **minimalistic approach** to building a Zettelkasten (or "second brain") in Obsidian. Over-engineering — by combining complex features from various sources — leads to "toxic perfectionism" and distracts from the actual work of writing and learning. A simple, gimmick-free setup is most effective.

---

## Key Benefits of the Zettelkasten Method

- **Forces you to slow down** — Abandoning speed-reading in favour of thorough notes leads to better retention and deeper understanding.
- **Instant feedback** — Writing out concepts is an immediate comprehension check; if you struggle to explain it, you haven't fully understood it.
- **Forms unexpected connections** — Storing notes in a single arena (rather than strict subject folders) lets ideas from different disciplines (biology, physics, history, etc.) intermingle and create *true wisdom*.
- **Accelerates writing** — Instead of starting research from scratch, you pull from a reusable collection of pre-written notes that naturally cluster around your interests.

---

## The Obsidian Vault Structure

The workflow uses **one Vault** (stored locally or on a cloud drive) organised into six folders:

| # | Folder | Purpose |
|---|--------|---------|
| 1 | **Rough Notes** | Temporary notepad for quick ideas or daily habit tracking |
| 2 | **Source Material** | Initial log of insights from books, videos, and articles |
| 3 | **Tags** | Empty notes used as tags (e.g. a note titled `humility`); other notes link to these |
| 4 | **Indexes** | Created when a tag accumulates too many notes; acts as a structured table of contents for a topic |
| 5 | **Templates** | A basic structural template (date, tags, title, references) pasted into new notes via hotkey |
| 6 | **Main Notes (Zettelkasten)** | Central folder for every finalised, independent "atomic note" |

---

## The Note-Taking Workflow

### 1. Source Material Notes
While consuming content, record:
- Page number or timestamp
- A relevant quote
- Your own elaboration on the idea (avoids passive learning)

### 2. Main Notes
Translate source ideas into **independent, self-explanatory atomic notes**:
- Focus on the bigger picture
- Keep under **500 words**
- Cover **a single idea only** — keeps notes flexible for future use

### 3. Tagging
- Apply a few **highly relevant** tags based on personal interests
- Avoid tags that are too vague *or* too specific
- Use **status tags** to track note maturity:
  - `#baby` — early, undeveloped idea
  - `#child` — growing, partially developed
  - `#adult` — fully formed, mature note

### 4. Referencing
At the bottom of each main note:
- Hyperlink back to the **original source material**
- Hyperlink to **related notes** with similar ideas

This weaves each new thought directly into the wider knowledge network.
