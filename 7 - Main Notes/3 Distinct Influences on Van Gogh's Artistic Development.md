**Task:** Write a short essay (500-700 words) discussing **three** distinct influences on Van Gogh's artistic development, drawing evidence from your readings and museum notes.

**Structure (Three Body Paragraphs):**
1. **Social/Thematic Influence: The Life of the Working Class.** (Focus on the Netherlands and his moral purpose.)
2. **Technical/Stylistic Influence: The Synthesis of _Japonisme_.** (Focus on composition, flatness, and color clarity.)
3. **Intellectual/Peer Influence: Paris and the Color Revolution.** (Focus on the shift from dark Dutch tones to bright, expressive Parisian colors, citing interaction with contemporaries like Monticelli.)

## **Essay**
Van Gogh's artistic journey was a radical ten-year transformation, moving from academic realism to expressive modernity. This evolution was profoundly shaped by his commitment to painting the working class, his adaptation of Japanese woodblock art compositions, and his immersion in Parisian color theory. In this essay, I will be mainly referencing to the Letters from Vincent Van Gogh and various artworks.

To understand Van Gogh's affinity with the working class, we need to see how he first started. In the Hague (early 1882), Van Gogh established a studio at the Schenkweg with the help of his brother Theo and started taking formal lessons from Anton Mauve on painting in watercolours and in oil. Having read Sensier's biography of Jean-Francois Millet, Van Gogh resolved to also become a painter of peasant life. In Van Gogh's letters during the mid-to-late 1885 period, Van Gogh argues that showing "the peasant figure in action... is the very essence of modern art" because it was ignored by classical and traditional schools. By following the working class around, from their cottages to the fields, the process required to paint peasants involves a longer, more tiring journey than the one so many painters of exotic subjects. Van Gogh believed that the current situation then, did not lead to any new discoveries in the art world, "a Parisian who has learned his drawing at the academy, will always convey the limbs and structure of the body in the same way". A prominent artwork that was produced during this time was ***The Potato Eaters***. From his letters to Theo, he describes the heads of the peasants had "the colour of a good dusty potato", and the whole was done in "copper and green-soap-like tones", and it should be "set off by putting something coloured a deep gold or copper round it". Van Gogh wanted to express how he felt after witnessing the lives of peasants, that "these people eating potatoes by the light of their lamp have dug the earth with the self-same hands they are now putting into the dish, and it thus suggests manual labour and - a meal honestly earned".

Van Gogh first recorded decorating his room with "a lot of small Japanese prints" while in Antwerp (late 1885). In his letters to Theo, he envies "the Japanese the extreme clarity of everything in their work ... Their work is as simple as breathing, and they do a figure in a few sure strokes as if it were as easy as doing up your waistcoat". As much as he was dazzled by the colourful palettes from the Impressionists, he was as inspired by Japanese woodblock print, where he starts to "look to things with a more Japanese eye" and "experience colors differently". In his later paintings like ***Vincent's Bedroom*** when he was in Arles (early 1888), Van Gogh stated in his letters that the shadows were omitted and it was "painted in bright flat tints like the Japanese prints", creating an "overall impression of rest or sleep". His adoption of Ukiyo-e principles included stylised contours and expressive compositions, and he dedicated himself to making drawings "in the manner of Japanese prints". Much like the analysis that I did on ***Self Portrait in Front of an Easel***, having a part of Van Gogh's body cut off, shows the ukiyo-e influence on him from Japanese woodblock prints as well, not just colour, but subject matter portrayal as well.

A little bit of art history: 1886 was a turning point as it saw the eighth and last group exhibition by the Impressionists and also witnessed the launch of the Neo-Impressionist movement by Seurat and Signac. Van Gogh was particularly drawn towards Seurat's pointillism, the use of separate dots of unmixed color. I also refer to this in my analysis of ***Van Gogh's Self Portrait In Front of an Easel***, "Instead of dots, the texture created on his shirt is created using small controlled lines that are similar in width but with various angles". Van Gogh also started experimenting on new colour theories, "seeking oppositions of blue with orange, red and green, yellow and violet seeking broken and neutral tones to harmonise brutal extremes". It is also during this time when Van Gogh's ideals for his art surfaces. Van Gogh wishes for his art to "say something consoling ... to paint men and women with a touch of the eternal, whose symbol was once the halo, which we try to convey by the very radiance and vibrancy of our colouring". Just like The Potato Eaters, Van Gogh felt the same way towards ***The Night Cafe***. He tried to express the idea that the cafe is a place where anyone can do anything, even something villainous. Van Gogh used "a contrast between soft pink with blood-red and wine-red, soft Louis XV-green and Veronese green with yellow-greens and harsh blue-greens, all this in an atmosphere of an infernal furnace in pale sulphur, to express the powers of darkness in a common tavern". The primary colours of the artwork, accompanied by the concentric swirls of the lamps, seem to warn us about this cafe. Another example is ***The Reaper*** which Van Gogh writes to Theo, "it is an image of death as the great book of nature speaks of ... and it is smiling". Ironically, he is hoping to cheer Theo up with his artwork, and the artwork represents hope. Van Gogh writes in his letter that he hopes "that the family will be for you what nature, the clods of earth, the grass, the yellow wheat, the peasant, are for me". This ties back to his belief that genuine art is defined by "what makes us sit up and think and what touches us as human beings".

These combined influences—Millet's moral realism, the compositional clarity of Ukiyo-e, and the technical mastery of modern colour found in Paris—collectively drove Van Gogh’s transformation into the artist known for fusing deep human emotion with intense, vibrating colour.

3. **Technical/Stylistic Influence (_Ukiyo-e_) (A):** This is strong and tightly argued. You move beyond simple description of prints to analyze their **functional impact** on his composition (cropping) and his aesthetic goals (clarity, flat tints in _Vincent's Bedroom_).

    - **Emotional Function:** You successfully argue that his color choices are not random but serve a specific emotional and expressive purpose.
