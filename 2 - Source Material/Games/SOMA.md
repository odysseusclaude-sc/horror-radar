**Introduction**
After 8.24 hours, I finally finished SOMA on the Steam Deck. SOMA is a science fiction survival horror game released in 2015 by Frictional Games, the developers behind the Amnesia series. This also reminds me that I have not played the Amnesia series... The sound design is what scares me the most. There is always some random clink or clank in the background while I am crouch walking, and it always makes me turn around. There aren't any jump scares in this, surprisingly. Horror games are so hauntingly beautiful: the atmosphere, the lighting, the blood-stained walls, the random howls along the alleyways. But this... this brought up some thalassophobia when it was revealed that the story takes place in a deep-sea research facility for the first time. I had to walk on the ocean bed, and worse, run away from some giant undersea worm controlled by an artificial intelligence entity. I can't believe a game like this was released ten years ago. To this day, it holds up, just like Alien: Isolation.

**Summary**:
The year is 2015, and our main protagonist is Simon Jarrett, a bookstore employee from Toronto. Simon was involved in a car accident and has since been suffering from brain damage. He goes to a doctor to get a brain scan, in hopes of building a digital model of his brain to test potential treatments to repair it. Once the brain scan is completed, he suddenly wakes up in a dark room, which looks like a research facility.

As we gather our bearings, we not only find out that we have time-traveled to 2104, but that the surface of the Earth has been wiped out by a comet. Humanity had long known about this apocalyptic-level threat and thus built this underwater facility in hopes of saving any last remnant of our species.

As we continue exploring the facility, Simon makes contact with Catherine Chun, a scientist from the facility who is now a digital scan inside a robot. She is also the person responsible for the Ark project. The Ark is a digital capsule that contains the brain scans of millions of people, simulating a paradise where they can live forever. The goal is to launch the Ark into space to allow humanity to survive among the stars. This starts our journey deeper into the research facility to retrieve the Ark and complete the launch.

This is also when we encounter the WAU, an artificial intelligence entity that was programmed to preserve humanity. After humanity was wiped out from the surface, the WAU strove to preserve humanity, regardless of what it took. It began by forcibly fusing people with machinery and structure gel, creating undying, eternally suffering monstrosities. Next, brain scans.

Simon slowly learns that he is not the Simon Jarrett from 2015, but a legacy brain scan from 2015. The real Simon Jarrett had already passed away in 2015, and we are one of the monsters created by the WAU.

To reach the abyss where the Ark is hidden, Simon needs a pressure suit. The only way to "move" him into the new suit is to copy his mind into it. This is the first time when I got the chills. All along, the "Coin Toss" theory, developed by Simon Jarrett to conceptualize the transfer of consciousness, was thought to be a physical transfer in a sense. The theory was that when a brain scan is used to upload into a new body, there is a 50/50 chance of the "real" consciousness transferring to the new vessel. In actual fact, we are being copied. After the scan, when we wake up in the new body, we hear our old body still making some noise. There is no transfer; only a copy.

Simon and Catherine then reach the final destination to launch the Ark. Simon straps into a pilot seat, believing that this time, the "transfer" will take him to the Ark. The launch is successful, and the Ark is sent into space. Yet, we open our eyes and realize that we are still at the bottom of the ocean. Catherine tries to explain that they were copied but lost the coin toss. Catherine reaches the stress threshold limit and self-destructs, leaving Simon all alone in eternal darkness. Meanwhile, we wake up in the Ark, and the world is lush green with waves crashing against the shore, and the credits roll.


Ship of Theseus
Good read: [Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/philosophy/comments/15hnlw/the_ship_of_theseus_paradox/)
If a ship has all its parts gradually replaced over time, is the resulting ship still the same original ship? Simon-1 is the 2015 Simon when he goes in for the brain scan. Simon-2 is the Simon that we initially wake up as, and is left behind when we "jump" into a pressure suit. Simon-3 is the Simon that is in the pressure suit and explores the research facility in the deep sea. Simon-3 is also the Simon that is left behind after launching the Ark. Simon-4 is the Simon that wakes up on the Ark, thinking that he made it. 

In SOMA, humanity is defined by our minds, and not our physical bodies. That's also why the WAU removes the constraint of the body at the start and tries to transplant the crews' minds into machines. If we are looking at Simon as a whole, with both his mind and body, Simon-1 is the real Simon. Following this idea, Simon-2, 3 and 4 are copies of Simon-1 and are not Simon-1 because the bodies they inhabit are anatomically different. The complexity comes in if we only define humanity by its mind. Technically, all Simons exist concurrently, Simon-1 excluded. But Simon, as who he is, defined by his brain, exist as a long-standing continuity. Simon-2 is still stuck in the research facility surrounded by monsters but he still exists. Simon-3 is also stuck in the research facility, all alone plunged in eternal darkness after Catherine short circuited. Simon-4 is living his best life on the Ark, reunited with a copy of Catherine. And all of them retain their memories.

Simon-1 = 100% brain, 100% human body
Simon-2 = 100% brain, 100% android body
Simon-3 = 100% brain, 100% pressure suit
Simon-4 = 100% brain

At which point or at what level will Simon be different? It's clear from SOMA that they are definitely different but what if Simon-2 was transferred into another body, that is identical, the difference being that it is not damage? I mean technically, even saying that Simon-1 to Simon-4 having 100% brain does not make sense because each of their brains will be going through different experiences. Well, am I the same person as I was before playing SOMA? Definitely not. I wouldn't be writing about this if not for SOMA. 

The Ark
Minor point here but SOMA feels more like a tragedy to me than horror. Humanity is about to be wiped out and the only 2 choices are to die or get copied into a digital simulation and then what? Whisked away to the stars, hoping to get picked up by some extra-terrestrial life? How long can the simulation run on its own without breaking? Hundreds or thousands of years? That in itself is also a hell. Would I have chosen to be on the Ark? My physical body still gets smashed into pieces but my brain lives on. Hmm... Am I defined by my thoughts or by my body or both?
