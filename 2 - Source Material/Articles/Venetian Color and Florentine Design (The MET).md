Summary
- In Florence, *disegno*, that "drawing" or “design,” was viewed as the essential beginning of artistic endeavor, the primary means for making art approximate nature. In Venice, *colorito*, “coloring”—not only color but also its judicious application—was deemed fundamental to conceiving painted images charged with the look of life.
Drawing
- The act of drawing was not only the art of using line to define form: it was the artistic underpinning of a work whereby an artist could <mark style="background: #CEE3F3;">express his inner vision</mark>. This was done to study anatomy and movement where they <mark style="background: #CEE3F3;">developed compositions in detailed drawings</mark> before transferring them to surfaces prepared for painting.
Coloring
- Venetian artists gradually softened their coloring until their manner equaled nature. Rather than beginning with careful drawings, Venetian painters often worked out compositions directly on the canvas, using <mark style="background: #CEE3F3;">layered patches of coloured brushstrokes rather than line</mark> to define form. Venetian drawings show an interest in how light will affect a body and how color will describe it in a painting.
