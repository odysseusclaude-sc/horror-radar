**Essay**
Facing an easel, Van Gogh holds a bundle of paint brushes, with a palette of messy colors balanced on his left arm. Looking at the bleu de travail, I can see traces of inspiration from Georges Seurat's (1859-91) pointillism. Pointillism is about using small brush dabs to create a mosaic without compromising on color intensity. Instead of dots, the texture created on his shirt is created using small controlled lines that are similar in width but with various angled contours. Van Gogh has intentionally created a complementary contrast between the overall dominant blue color of the jacket and the yellowish orange pointillism dabs. This creates a movement effect, with the back ends of the strokes representing kinetic energy. I imagine the individual brushstrokes to be atoms of his jacket swirling around him, visual vibration. Brushstrokes orbiting his body like planets around a sun, making Van Gogh the center of the universe (painting). In between Van Gogh's reddish beard and bleu de travail, his neck acts as a contrast between the cool blue against the cement grey wall. This then leads our gaze to Van Gogh's face, with his serious gaze. Van Gogh's hair is created with less controlled brushstrokes, in the sense that the brushstrokes for his hair seem to go in all directions. This gives his head a spiky texture, along with how the oil paint looks on canvas, gives his face a rough and tough impression. This, along with his gaze, gives Van Gogh a dignified look. A man of discipline. 

The color of the easel imitates the same color in real life and cuts off the left part of Van Gogh's body. Japanese paintings would have their figures cut off by something, be it a curtain or a print. Having a part of Van Gogh's body cut off, shows the ukiyo-e influence on him from Japanese woodblock prints. The backdrop of a wall does not have any depth to it, it is simply a flat and matte grey surface. This pushes Van Gogh as a painter, the subject matter, into the audience's eye. 

Overall, this self portrait shows the impression Van Gogh has of himself. A culmination of the ukiyo-e influence Japanese woodblocks had on him, the complementary contrast in color, and Seurat's pointillism.
