## Introduction
What the impressionists strived for: in technique, to reproduce the broad atmospheric effects of outdoor light; in sentiment, to convey the clarity of the immediate sensation. The Impressionists wanted to break away from having to always base a painting on a story (e.g. Greek mythology) and its link to historical and religious subjects. The group chose landscapes because it appealed to only observation, rather than imagination, which allowed them to express their individual views of nature. The Impressionists believed that it was more important to paint what one saw, rather than how they were taught. This also gave rise to plein air painting. Coincidentally, this was also what Vincent Van Gogh came up with while painting peasant life.

## [[Édouard Manet]]
**Role**: Édouard Manet had invented the colors that the future impressionists were trying to find in living nature.

**1863**: The traditional Salon and the Salon des Refusés opened simultaneously. Previously, the only way for artists to exhibit their paintings was through the Salon but the judging criteria was very strict. Emperor Napoleon III personally toured the exhibition rooms and was so shocked by how strict the criteria that he ordered all rejected artworks to be exhibited. This was when [[Luncheon on the Grass - Édouard Manet]] was exhibited.

**1864**: When Manet arrived in Boulogne, life threw him the gift of a marvelous subject: in the Channel off Cherbourg a battle was taking place between two American ships, the federal corvette called the "Kearsarge" and the Confederate ship called the "Alabama". [[The Battle of the Kearsarge and the Alabama - Édouard Manet]] may be considered his first historical painting.

**1865**: [[Olympia - Édouard Manet]] was exhibited and everything in this painting caused indignation, beginning with the title on the frame. The name given to the painting defied classical tradition.

**1866**: [[The Fifer - Édouard Manet]] was painted and it was the first painting to have used color to render the surrounding air. The idea that one could create a painting solely for pictorial reasons (as Manet had done with the Fifer) was foreign to people living in the nineteenth century. Manet was the first nineteenth-century painter to let himself be guided primarily by color and light when composing his works, which is why he became a model and a master for the future Impressionists.

**1867**: Courbet opened his own independent pavilion. Manet, too, decided to build a temporary shelter next to Courbet’s exhibition at the corner of avenues Montaigne and Alma, not far from the Universal Exposition. Émile Zola spoke of Manet as a painter who stayed close to nature, placing his confidence solely in the observation of nature, rather than science or experience.

[[The Execution of Maximilian - Édouard Manet]] was painted as Manet was deeply moved by the tragedy. He was inclined towards the history of his own era, rather than subjects from antique mythology and the Bible, which was commonly found in the Salon. It was also inspired by [[The Execution of 3 May 1808 in Madrid - Francisco Goya]]. Manet also learned how to achieve the emotional tension of Goya’s work.

**1868**: [[The Portrait of Émile Zola - Édouard Manet]] was painted to express gratitude for his continuous support. 

**1870**: Napoléon III declared war against Prussia. On 1 September, the French army, led by its emperor, surrendered in Sedan. A republic was declared in France. Manet joined the national guard as an artilleryman.

**1871**: Manet only returned to Paris around the end of June, 1871, after the semaine sanglante, or “bloody week” was over. He no longer had the financial means to support his art. Fortunately, the art dealer Durand-Ruel purchased twenty-four paintings from him that he proceeded to show in various exhibitions.

**1872**: With Paris recovering from the war, Manet’s group relocated to a café called La Nouvelle Athènes, on place Pigalle. Not all the regulars from the café Guerbois made the transition. Bazille had been killed in the war; Monet and Sisley were living in the suburbs; and Pissarro only stopped by when he found himself in Paris. Cézanne’s rare appearances were seen as theatre.

1873: [[Masked Ball at the Opera - Édouard Manet]] marked the end of Manet's plein-air impressionist period and his return to urban motifs.

**1874**: Manet spent the summer 1874 at his family property in Gennevilliers, near Paris. Claude Monet, whom Manet had helped to find a house after the war in 1871, was staying at Argenteuil. For Manet, visiting the Monet family was as simple as crossing the bridge over the Seine.

Painted his first impressionist painting, [[Boating - Édouard Manet]], and his second impressionist painting, [[Argenteuil - Édouard Manet]].

**1875**: Manet visited Venice with his wife and the painter James Tissot. From this trip, we only have two views of the Grand Canal, painted during the final days.

**1877**: Painted [[Nana - Édouard Manet]] and was exhibited on the opening day of the Salon.

**1878**: Théodore Duret published his first serious work: History of the Impressionist Painters. He deduced that Manet was born a painter the way one is born with perfect pitch. He saw his future painting in colors the same way Michelangelo felt his yet unformed sculpture in a block of stone. And whatever Manet painted, color alone was both an end and a means.

**1881**: Manet was already ill at this time. Physicians were prescribing various treatments for him, sending him to water cures, but his health continued to deteriorate. Manet was made chevalier of the Legion of Honor in December.

**1882**: Manet turns 50 and Parisians went to see his final painting at the Salon, [[A Bar at the Folies-Bergère - Édouard Manet]]. Manet spent the summer of 1882 with his family in Rueil. Already nearly immobilized by the illness, he was painting sunny landscapes and floral bouquets in the garden after nature.

**1883**: A medical examination in April revealed the need to amputate Manet’s left leg due to gangrene. The amputation took place on 20 April and ten days later, on 30 April, the day before the opening of the new Salon, Manet passed away.

## [[Claude Monet]]

**Role**: Monet chose a single genre for himself, landscape painting, and in that he achieved a degree of perfection none of his contemporaries managed to attain.

Eugene Boudin advised Monet to stop doing caricatures and take up landscapes. Boudin was the the one who passed on to Monet his conviction of the importance of working in open air, which Monet then transmit to his Impressionist friends.

**1854**: Monet goes to Paris for two months and extends his stay. Monet attends a private school, L’Académie Suisse, and met the future Impressionist Camille Pissarro.

**1861**: Monet left for Algeria with the African regiment. 

**1862**: Claude's parents sent him to attend classes with a Parisian painter, Augeste Toulmouche, a Monet family relative. Toulmouche thought it essential that Monet attend the free studio run by his own teacher, Charles Gleyre. It was in Gleyre's studio that Monet met Pierre Auguste Renoir, Alfred Sisley and Frédéric Bazille.

Monet had the knack of persuading bourgeois Parisians to commission him and Renoir to do their portraits, and in this way he managed to pay for the group studio, the model and coal for the heating. Frédéric Bazille had money, and with the money his parents sent, he rented a studio for himself, Money, Renoir and Sisley. Pissarro brought Cézanne along with him. For some time this studio became their meeting place. They had stopped attending Gleyre’s studio, and together they now left to work in the region favored by the Barbizon School painters, the Fontainebleau forest.

In the Fontainebleau forest, Monet first had a revelation of the richness of the color effects created by the sun filtering through the leaves.

**1865**: Monet began to paint [[Luncheon on the Grass - Claude Monet]], inspired by Manet's Luncheon on the Grass. 

**1866**: Monet painted the portrait of Camille Doncieux, his future wife, [[Woman in the Green Dress - Claude Monet]]. 

**1867**: Monet's father ordered him to spend the summer at Sainte-Adresse under his aunt’s surveillance to keep him away from Camille, who was just about to give birth to their first son, Jean. His father threatened to withdraw financial support completely if he married. This was when Monet was in such a state of nervous agitation that he began to lose his vision. He painted a series of landscapes at Sainte-Adresse, [[Regattas at Sainte-Adresse - Claude Monet]], [[Terrace at Sainte-Adresse - Claude Monet]] and [[Woman in the Garden - Claude Monet]]. Monet had already begun to analyse the colours he saw and had discovered what Delacroix, in his time, had found. It was that black and white do not exist in nature. There is colour everywhere, one only needs to be able to see it. For that purpose it is essential to educate, to train one’s eye, and Monet made a start on this difficult task.

**1868**: Monet marries Camille Doncieux.

**1870**: During the Franco-Prussian War, Monet travels to London, where he meets Camille Pissarro and Charles-François Daubigny. Daubigny introduces him to the dealer Paul Durand-Ruel.

**1871:** Returns to France via Holland and settles in Argenteuil on the banks of the Seine. Following Daubigny’s example, Monet built a floating studio for himself. Since Monet often painted out in the middle of the river, the viewer is situated inside the landscape in these paintings.

**1872**: Monet paints [[Regattas at Argenteuil - Claude Monet]].
